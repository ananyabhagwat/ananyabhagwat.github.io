Pollygraph: An Interactive Polling Plug-in

A proposed system that lets participants cast votes by pointing at regions of a shared display, replacing hand-raising with spatial, anonymous, and continuous participation.

Overview

This project explores alternative ways of conducting live classroom polls and collective feedback on shared displays. Commonly used methods such as hand-raising and online polling tools rely on social visibility, personal devices, and internet connectivity, and tend to reduce responses to discrete categories.

Pollygraph investigates whether a spatial interaction model could enable more fluid and engaged participation. The proposed system allows participants to cast votes by pointing at regions of a shared display during a designated voting moment. Votes are visualised collectively as spatial distributions, supporting continuous rather than discrete expressions of opinion.

User Research

Research was conducted in two phases. The first involved four semi-structured interviews - two with students and two with instructors - focusing on experiences with live polls, hand-raising, and other participation mechanisms in classrooms.

The second phase used autoethnography, drawing on my own experiences as a student in large lectures to observe moments of disengagement, participation anxiety, and delays caused by discrete voting methods.

Pain Points

Two dominant pain points emerged. With hand-raising, participants identified fear of judgement from peers, fear of being called upon, and a tendency to go along with the majority regardless of personal opinion.

Online polling platforms introduced a different set of problems: dependence on patchy internet, the distraction of getting phones out mid-lecture, time consumed by scanning codes and entering responses, and a break in lecture flow that undermined the engagement they were supposed to create.

Design Process

The design process followed a Research - Define - Ideate - Refine structure. The ideate phase used rapid sketching to generate eight concepts, of which two were selected for prototyping: a laser-pointer device paired with a board that provides dynamic spatial feedback, and a local-network voting platform that sidesteps internet connectivity issues.

Low-fi Prototyping

For the laser-pointer concept, I used Wizard of Oz prototyping. A chart paper was divided into spatial areas and participants were asked to point a torch at regions representing their choices. I then replaced the blank chart with one covered in coloured sticky notes indicating vote density.

Wizard of Oz prototype - torch and chart paper Click to add image

Feedback highlighted intuitive engagement and visual immediacy, but raised concerns about accuracy, feasibility at scale, and the distraction of laser pointers in classrooms. For the local-network platform, I created low-fidelity screen mock-ups. In the end, 5 out of 6 participants preferred the pointer concept because it was novel, engaging, and disrupted the flow of the session less.

High-fi Prototype

Moving from the laser-based concept to a remote pointer interaction integrated directly into presentation software, the final prototype takes the form of a PowerPoint plug-in. Instructors can mark specific slides as Pollygraph-enabled. Voting is activated only when those slides are presented, ensuring participation is time-bounded and does not disrupt teaching flow.

Hi-fi prototype - PowerPoint plug-in interface Click to add image

The plug-in provides configurable voting layouts and visualisation options - spatial regions such as binary splits, quadrants, and continuous axes, as well as dynamic outputs including density maps, counters, and bar charts.

Reflection

The proposed system assumes the availability of a shared display capable of receiving multiple simultaneous pointer inputs. While such capabilities exist in some interactive displays and smart boards, this limits immediate deployability. The project therefore prioritises interaction design and classroom experience over backend feasibility.

A key design decision was to embed voting within presentation software rather than create a standalone system, ensuring that participation remains instructor-controlled and does not interrupt teaching flow. Pollygraph functions as a speculative but grounded exploration of how rethinking interaction modalities can open new possibilities for collective participation in educational settings.

Independent Project

Revisiting the Baggage Reclaim System

Redesigning the information environment at baggage reclaim to reduce uncertainty and frustration during the waiting stage, without changing how baggage handling actually works.

Type Independent
Methods Survey, Qualitative interviews, Heuristic evaluation, Journey mapping
Output Information system design proposal
  • Overview
  • User Research
  • Journey Map
  • Heuristic Evaluation
  • Final Design
  • Reflection

Overview

This project explores ways to improve the passenger experience during baggage reclaim, focusing on reducing uncertainty and frustration while waiting for luggage. The design proposes a coordinated information system: staff can update the unloading phase via a simple interface, and passengers receive clear, real-time updates on displays, including historical averages and guidance for delayed baggage.

By redesigning the flow of information and the waiting environment, the project aims to make baggage reclaim more predictable, transparent, and less stressful, without altering airport operations.

User Research

To understand the challenges passengers face, I conducted a mixed-methods study combining a survey and qualitative interviews. The goal was to capture both quantitative measures of frustration and personal experiences, identifying where uncertainty, waiting, and physical effort create stress.

Common issues included uncertainty about bag arrival, lack of guidance on delays, physical difficulty lifting heavy luggage, and crowding at the carousel. These insights highlighted opportunities to improve information visibility, waiting area organisation, and guidance for delayed or missing bags.

Journey Map

Mapping the passenger journey from arrivals through to collecting bags revealed five distinct stages, each with its own failure modes: finding the correct belt, waiting at the carousel with no status updates, identifying bags among similar-looking luggage, physically retrieving bags from a crowded belt, and dealing with delayed or missing bags with no guidance on next steps.

Heuristic Evaluation

The heuristic evaluation highlighted multiple pain points, but visibility during the waiting stage stood out as the most critical - scoring a severity of 5/5. Passengers experience uncertainty, lack of status updates, crowding, and physical strain during this stage. By targeting this phase, the design can have the greatest impact without needing to change airport operations.

Heuristic evaluation table Click to add image

Based on the evaluation, five design requirements were identified: real-time updates on unloading phase, instructions for delayed or missing bags, reorganised waiting areas to reduce crowding, a simple staff interface for updating bag status, and messaging that minimises stress and uncertainty.

Final Design

A central carousel display provides live, phase-based updates on unloading progress, alongside contextual information drawn from previous similar flights to help set realistic expectations. Clear messaging is introduced for prolonged waits, guiding passengers on next steps if a bag has not arrived.

The physical waiting area is subtly restructured. A marked standing zone set back from the carousel encourages passengers to step away from the belt until their bag appears, improving visibility, reducing crowding, and making the space safer to share.

Reflection

The changes proposed improve waiting experience through clearer information and better use of space, rather than changes to baggage handling itself. The system gives airlines and unloaders the freedom to decide the method of unloading and does not add communication overhead to their workflow.

The constraint of not altering airport operations was a productive one. It forced the design to work within existing infrastructure and staff workflows, which made the solution more realistic and immediately deployable than a full system redesign would have been.

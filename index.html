Interaction Design — Group Project

Chippity: Reflective LLM Use in Higher Education

A browser extension that makes the hidden costs of ChatGPT use visible — cognitive and environmental — without getting in the way of why students use it.

Course Interaction Design
Methods Interviews, Thematic Analysis, Prototyping, User Evaluation
Team 5 people
  • Overview
  • Research
  • Design Process
  • Low-fi Prototype
  • High-fi Prototype
  • Outcomes

Overview

LLM use in higher education is growing fast, and the interfaces students use offer no support for thinking critically about that use. Every prompt draws on energy-intensive infrastructure, and sustained reliance on LLM output reorganises how students think, weakening the conditions for independent reasoning. Students are aware of both. They still don't change their behaviour. Chippity is a browser extension with a companion mobile app designed to address that gap, not by restricting use, but by making its costs visible and actionable at the point of interaction.

The research question driving the project was whether cognitive dependence and environmental impact could be made perceptible within existing LLM workflows, without undermining the convenience that makes students use them in the first place.

Research

The design process began with 15 semi-structured interviews with regular LLM users. Reflexive thematic analysis produced four themes: convenience normalises patterned reliance, students have genuine concerns about losing cognitive agency, environmental impact is invisible and weakly motivating, and there is a persistent gap between intention and behaviour. Students could articulate what they should do differently. Nothing in the interface gave them a moment to act on it.

Design Process

Those findings shaped the requirements directly. Any intervention had to work within the moment of prompting, had to preserve students' sense of control, and had to fit into existing study routines without adding friction. A Crazy 8s workshop generated directions including wearables, standalone GPT interfaces, and goal-setting dashboards. The screening criterion was simple: does this require leaving ChatGPT? Anything that did was discarded. A browser extension was the only option that kept reflection inside the interface students already use.

Low-fi Prototype + First Evaluation

The low-fidelity prototype included pre-set profiles, a settings page, 15 pop-up nudge variants tested across different screen positions, personal usage statistics, and community features. Testing with 5 participants revealed that tone mattered more than content. Nudges that felt prescriptive or moralising were dismissed. Nudges framed as invitations to reflect were rated consistently helpful. All participants placed the system closer to helpful than intrusive.

High-fi Prototype + Second Evaluation

The high-fidelity prototype introduced four profiles (Eco Mode, Think Mode, Smart Balance, Custom), 18 refined prompts, a mascot for personal engagement, and a community dashboard showing cohort-level usage. A second round of in-person testing with 4 participants produced further refinements to font size, button logic, settings clarity, and hover descriptions.

Outcomes

The final system demonstrates that reflective scaffolding can be embedded within existing LLM workflows without sacrificing convenience. The community feature produced the strongest response across both evaluations. Knowing peers were attempting similar goals made individual effort feel worth sustaining. The project produced three contributions: a qualitative account of how students experience cognitive offloading and environmental invisibility, a working system introducing a reflective layer over existing LLM interfaces, and a set of design principles for responsible LLM use in higher education.

HCI for Health and Wellbeing — Individual

A GQM Evaluation of Clue

A systematic audit of the menstrual tracking app Clue, asking whether it actually supports the goals users bring to it — or just the ones it was designed around.

Course HCI for Health and Wellbeing
Methods GQM Framework, Heuristic Audit, Literature Analysis
Framework Lin et al., CHI 2024
  • Overview
  • The Paper
  • From DRs to Criteria
  • Findings
  • Equity and Safety
  • Design Goals

Overview

Menstrual tracking apps are mostly glorified calendars. They predict when your next period is and let you log symptoms. What they don't do is help you understand your data. This audit asked whether Clue, one of the most popular apps in the space, actually meets the needs of the people using it.

The audit was grounded in Lin et al. (CHI 2024), who studied 30 people to find out what they actually want from a tracking app. The short version: they want to anticipate upcoming symptoms, spot patterns across multiple signals, judge whether their cycle is normal for them, and learn from their data over time.

The Paper

Lin et al. turned their findings into five design requirements. The problem is that design requirements from academic papers are broad by design. They're categories, not criteria. Making them auditable was the first task.

Lin et al. design probes — MenstrualMate and PeriodBubble Click to add image

From Design Requirements to Testable Criteria

I derived four design implications from the paper, each tied to a specific user goal, and from those produced nine binary requirements: concrete, verifiable statements about what the interface must do. Each requirement had a specific metric, a thing you could either find on screen or couldn't. This translation was the core of the work.

GQM matrix — full requirements and metrics table Click to add image

Findings

Clue passes three of nine requirements. It lets you track multiple symptoms, shows you which symptoms tend to co-occur, and links to medical content when something looks atypical. It fails on prediction uncertainty (it gives you one date, no range), multi-signal comparison (you can't overlay two symptoms on a graph), and cross-cycle reflection (there is no way to compare this cycle to the last one in any meaningful view).

Clue — failing metric M1.2 Click to add image

Equity and Safety

The audit also looked at equity and safety, because the failures aren't just usability problems. The features that would actually support sensemaking are behind a subscription. Predictions are shown without any clinical disclaimer and can easily be read as diagnostic. And the app never tells you when its analysis is insufficient, which means users can build incorrect beliefs about their own bodies without knowing it.

Design Goals

The audit concluded with three design goals: show predictions with visible uncertainty rather than false precision, support comparison across multiple signals with interpretive help, and enable cross-cycle reflection that doesn't rely on memory. These fed directly into the prototype project.

HCI for Health and Wellbeing — Individual

Menstrual Tracker Prototype

A functional prototype built to test whether uncertainty-aware predictions, multivariate exploration, and cross-cycle reflection actually help users make sense of their data.

Course HCI for Health and Wellbeing
Methods Figma Prototyping, Think-Aloud, Usability Study
Participants 5 (remote, think-aloud)
  • Overview
  • Design Decisions
  • Prototype Screens
  • Usability Study
  • Findings
  • Next Steps

Overview

This project picked up where the audit left off. Three design goals, and a prototype built to test whether they actually work. The prototype has four views: the Calendar, Symptoms, Analysis, and Information. Rather than redesigning Clue in full, it demonstrates how targeted interface decisions could better support menstrual health data exploration.

Design Decisions

Two decisions shaped the prototype throughout. The first was representing uncertainty honestly: a range of plausible dates rather than a fixed prediction, and gradients rather than point markers for symptom forecasts. The second was embedding contextual events (travel, stress, sleep) across all views, so users can see whether a change in symptoms coincides with something that happened in their life without having to cross-reference manually.

The calendar shows period start predicted across a five to six day range, with symptom predictions across upcoming days. The symptoms view lets you compare up to four signals, flags significant changes, and has a toggle to compare against previous cycles. The analysis view summarises cycle characteristics, highlights deviations, and links to specific explanations when something looks different. The information view surfaces relevant articles contextually when a deviation is detected.

Usability Study

A usability study was conducted remotely with five participants, three with prior tracking experience and two without. Participants completed five predefined tasks using think-aloud protocol, rated helpfulness per task on a seven-point scale, and completed a short exit interview. No personal health data was collected; all participants used the same example dataset.

Four of five tasks had majority completion. Helpfulness ratings ranged between 6 and 7 across tasks.

Task completion and results table Click to add image

Findings

Uncertainty windows worked. Every participant said they preferred a range to a fixed date and described it as more realistic.

The explanatory content didn't land. Not one participant opened the "how predictions work" link without being directed to it. The information was there; it just wasn't visible enough to find.

The biggest finding was that participants defaulted to the calendar for everything, including tasks better handled in the analysis or symptoms tabs. They'd spent years using apps where the calendar was the whole product, and that mental model didn't shift just because other views existed.

Next Steps

Make the prediction explanation prominent rather than tucked away. Use higher-contrast colours for deviation alerts. Make "learn more" links go to the specific explanation rather than a topic index. Replace cycle-day numbers with phase markers so cross-cycle comparison actually makes sense. Integrate contextual factors directly into deviation detection rather than leaving users to connect the dots themselves.

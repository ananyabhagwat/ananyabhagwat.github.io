Interaction Design / Group Project

Chippity: Reflective LLM Use in Higher Education

A browser extension that makes the hidden costs of ChatGPT use visible — cognitive and environmental — without getting in the way of why students use it.

Course Interaction Design
Methods Semi-structured interviews, Thematic analysis, Prototyping, User evaluation
Team 5 people

Overview

LLM use in higher education is growing fast, and the interfaces students use offer no support for thinking critically about that use. Every prompt draws on energy-intensive infrastructure, and sustained reliance on LLM output reorganises how students think, weakening the conditions for independent reasoning. Students are aware of both. They still don't change their behaviour. Chippity is a browser extension with a companion mobile app designed to address that gap, not by restricting use, but by making its costs visible and actionable at the point of interaction.

The research question driving the project was whether cognitive dependence and environmental impact could be made perceptible within existing LLM workflows, without undermining the convenience that makes students use them in the first place.

Research

The design process began with 15 semi-structured interviews with regular LLM users. Reflexive thematic analysis produced four themes: convenience normalises patterned reliance, students have genuine concerns about losing cognitive agency, environmental impact is invisible and weakly motivating, and there is a persistent gap between intention and behaviour.

Design Process

Those findings shaped the requirements directly. Any intervention had to work within the moment of prompting, had to preserve students' sense of control, and had to fit into existing study routines without adding friction. A Crazy 8s workshop generated directions including wearables, standalone GPT interfaces, and goal-setting dashboards.

The screening criterion was simple: does this require leaving ChatGPT? Anything that did was discarded. A browser extension was the only option that kept reflection inside the interface students already use.

Low-fi Prototype and First Evaluation

The low-fidelity prototype included pre-set profiles, a settings page, 15 pop-up nudge variants tested across different screen positions, personal usage statistics, and community features.

Testing with 5 participants revealed that tone mattered more than content. Nudges that felt prescriptive or moralising were dismissed. Nudges framed as invitations to reflect were rated consistently helpful. All participants placed the system closer to helpful than intrusive.

High-fi Prototype and Second Evaluation

The high-fidelity prototype introduced four profiles (Eco Mode, Think Mode, Smart Balance, Custom), 18 refined prompts, a mascot for personal engagement, and a community dashboard showing cohort-level usage.

A second round of in-person testing with 4 participants produced further refinements to font size, button logic, settings clarity, and hover descriptions.

Outcomes

The final system demonstrates that reflective scaffolding can be embedded within existing LLM workflows without sacrificing convenience. The community feature produced the strongest response across both evaluations. Knowing peers were attempting similar goals made individual effort feel worth sustaining.

The project produced three contributions: a qualitative account of how students experience cognitive offloading and environmental invisibility, a working system introducing a reflective layer over existing LLM interfaces, and a set of design principles for responsible LLM use in higher education.
